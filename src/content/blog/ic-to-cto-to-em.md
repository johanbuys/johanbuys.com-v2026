I've had three distinct careers in tech, all within the same 20 years. I started as an individual contributor writing code every day. Then I co-founded a startup and became a CTO. Now I'm an engineering manager at a Series B company. Each transition felt like starting over, and each one taught me something the previous role couldn't.

## The IC Years: Learning to Build

My first decade in tech was pure craftsmanship. I wrote C#, ASP.NET, jQuery, and eventually React. I rewrote legacy systems, built payment processing pipelines, and learned what it means to write code that other people depend on.

The most valuable lesson from this period wasn't technical; it was learning to care about the humans who use your software. Early in my career, I rewrote a legacy ASP system into a modern single-page application. The technical challenge was interesting, but what stuck with me was watching users who had struggled with the old system suddenly find their work easier. That feeling of making someone's day a little better through code never gets old.

## The Founder Years: Learning to Survive

In 2012, I co-founded Blue Boulder Trading as the sole technical founder. For nine years, I was the CTO, the architect, the DevOps team, and often the only developer. I built ML-powered computer vision systems, a SaaS agreement-signing platform, and a task management system while working directly with the CEO on product strategy.

Being a technical co-founder teaches you resourcefulness in a way nothing else can. When there's no DevOps team, you become the DevOps team. When the ML model isn't performing, you learn ML. When a customer needs a feature by Friday, you ship it by Thursday.

COVID killed the company. That experience was brutal but clarifying. It taught me that building great technology isn't enough; you need timing, market fit, and a bit of luck. It also taught me that I wanted to build teams, not just products.

## The Manager Years: Learning to Multiply

When I transitioned to management, the hardest adjustment was redefining what "productive" means. As an IC, productivity is tangible: you can count commits, features shipped, bugs fixed. As a manager, your output is your team's output. Your best days are often invisible.

At Cylinder Health, I built an engineering team from scratch. Hired every person. Established the culture. Created the processes. And I discovered something surprising: the skills I developed as a founder (scrappiness, systems thinking, comfort with ambiguity) were exactly what I needed as a manager at a growing startup.

The CTO experience gave me technical credibility that makes my management style possible. When I review architecture decisions, my team knows it's coming from someone who has built and operated systems at scale. When I push back on a timeline, they know I understand the technical complexity. This trust is the foundation of everything.

## What Each Role Got Wrong

As an IC, I undervalued communication. I thought great code spoke for itself. It doesn't. The best engineers I've managed are the ones who can explain their decisions clearly, not just implement them.

As a CTO, I undervalued delegation. I held on to too much for too long because I could do it faster myself. That's a trap that prevents both you and your team from growing.

As a manager, I initially undervalued patience. Not every engineer develops at the same pace, and not every team dynamic resolves itself quickly. The best outcomes often come from giving people time and space rather than intervening immediately.

## Why I Chose Management

People sometimes ask if I miss being an IC or a founder. The honest answer is that I still do both, just in different proportions. I still write code. I still think about architecture. I still have that founder's instinct to move fast and build scrappy solutions.

But the most satisfying moments in my career have been watching engineers I hired grow into roles they didn't think they were ready for. That's the multiplier effect of management: your impact scales through people, not just through code.

Twenty years in, I'm still building. I just build teams now.
