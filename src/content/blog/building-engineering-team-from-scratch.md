When I joined Cylinder Health in August 2023, there was no frontend engineering team. No established processes. No internship pipeline. Just a product that needed to grow and a mandate to build the team that would make it happen.

Two and a half years later, I've hired every member of a five-person engineering team, launched an internship program, promoted engineers into senior roles, and built a culture I'm genuinely proud of. Here's what I learned along the way.

## Hire for Curiosity, Not Just Skill

The temptation when building a team quickly is to optimize for immediate productivity. You want someone who can hit the ground running with your exact stack. But I've found that the best hires are the ones who are deeply curious, the ones who ask "why" more than "how."

Every engineer I hired had strong fundamentals, but what set them apart was their eagerness to learn. Some had never worked with our exact stack. That was fine. Curiosity compounds in ways that narrow expertise doesn't.

## Give Autonomy Early

One mistake I see managers make is waiting too long to give engineers ownership. They hover, review everything, and create bottlenecks in the name of quality control.

My approach is different. I provide the context (the why behind what we're building, the constraints, the expectations) and then I get out of the way. I still review every frontend flow that ships, but that's about ensuring consistency and catching edge cases, not about control.

The result is engineers who feel trusted, who take pride in their work, and who escalate problems early because they know I'll help rather than blame.

## Build Culture Intentionally

Culture doesn't happen by accident. At Cylinder, I established community-of-practice sessions where engineers share what they've learned: a new React pattern, a debugging technique, a conference talk that shifted their thinking. These sessions aren't mandatory, but attendance is consistently high because people genuinely want to learn from each other.

I also started an internship program, which forced us to think carefully about mentorship and documentation. Teaching someone from scratch reveals every gap in your processes. It made us better.

## Stay Technical

I still push code. I still review pull requests. I still get excited about a clever architectural decision. This isn't about proving anything; it's about making better decisions as a manager. When I understand the technical complexity of what my team is building, I can set more realistic timelines, have more productive architecture discussions, and earn the kind of respect that makes my feedback land differently.

## The Hardest Part

Honestly, the hardest part of building a team isn't the hiring or the processes. It's navigating the moments when someone is struggling and you need to figure out whether they need more support, more challenge, or a different role entirely. Those conversations require empathy, directness, and a genuine investment in someone's growth, even when the outcome isn't what either of you hoped for.

Building a team from scratch has been the most rewarding work of my career. Nothing beats watching someone you hired ship something they're proud of.
